How EV charging time is calculated

Charging time depends on three things: how much energy the battery needs (kWh), how fast the charger can deliver it (kW), and how efficiently the car's onboard charger converts AC to DC (efficiency η). The formula is:

Time (h) = Battery Capacity (kWh) × Charge Delta (%) ÷ (Charger Power (kW) × Efficiency η)

Worked example — Tesla Model 3 (75 kWh usable), charging from 15% to 80% on an 11 kW Level 2 wall box at 90% efficiency:

  1. Charge delta: 80% − 15% = 65% = 0.65
  2. Energy needed: 75 kWh × 0.65 = 48.75 kWh
  3. Effective power: 11 kW × 0.90 = 9.9 kW
  4. Time: 48.75 ÷ 9.9 = 4.92 hours (≈ 4 h 55 min)
Circuit note: An 11 kW Level 2 charger draws about 11 kW ÷ 240 V ≈ 45.8 A — you'll typically need a dedicated 50 A or 60 A circuit. To convert your charger's rated amps to kilowatts, use our amps to kW calculator.

Level 1 vs Level 2 vs DC Fast Charging

The three charging levels cover vastly different use cases. Level 1 is adequate for short daily commutes; Level 2 is the standard home and workplace solution; DC fast charging is for highway stops where speed matters. The table below shows typical power ranges and a rough full-charge estimate for a 60 kWh battery from 20% to 80%:

Level Power Range 60–80% range (20%→80%, 60 kWh) Typical Use Case
Level 1 (AC) 1.0–1.8 kW ~22–40 h Standard household outlet (NEMA 5-15). Overnight top-up for low daily mileage.
Level 2 (AC) 3.6–22 kW ~1.8–11 h Dedicated home wall box or public parking EVSE. Most practical for daily charging.
DC Fast Charge 50–350 kW ~15–72 min Highway corridor fast chargers (CCS, CHAdeMO, Tesla Supercharger). Highway stops only.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to charge an EV from 20% to 80%?

It depends on battery size and charger power. A common 60 kWh EV charged from 20% to 80% (a 36 kWh net charge) on an 11 kW Level 2 charger at 90% efficiency takes about 3.6 hours. On a 7.2 kW charger the same session takes roughly 5.6 hours. Use the calculator above for your specific values. The 20–80% range is recommended because the battery management system (BMS) charges at full speed throughout — above 80% the rate tapers.

Does EV charging slow down near 100%?

Yes. Above approximately 80% the BMS progressively reduces charge current to protect the lithium cells from stress, heat, and premature capacity loss. This is called the CC/CV (constant-current/constant-voltage) taper phase. From 80% to 100% the real-world charge time is typically 50–100% longer than a linear model predicts. This calculator provides a linear estimate most accurate for the 10–80% range.

What is the difference between Level 2 and DC fast charging?

Level 2 chargers supply AC power (typically 3.6–22 kW) that the car's onboard charger converts to DC before it enters the battery pack. Because the onboard charger is the bottleneck, even a 22 kW supply won't help a car whose onboard charger is rated for only 11 kW.

DC fast chargers (50–350 kW) bypass the onboard charger entirely and push DC directly into the pack. They can deliver far more power, but they also degrade the battery slightly faster over hundreds of sessions, and the BMS still limits peak power as the SoC rises above 80%. Our kW to amps calculator shows the enormous current differences involved.

Why is charging efficiency included in the formula?

During AC charging, the onboard charger's power electronics convert alternating current to direct current, dissipating some energy as heat. A typical AC onboard-charger efficiency is 85–95%, so 0.90 (90%) is a reasonable default. For DC fast charging most conversion happens in the charger station hardware; onboard losses are lower but thermal management still draws power — using 0.95 is appropriate for DC sessions. Lower efficiency means more grid energy is consumed and the actual charge time is longer.

Can I use this calculator for DC fast charging?

Yes, with a caveat: DC fast chargers are rated by their peak output, but actual delivered power varies with battery temperature, state of charge, and the car's own limits. Your average power during a fast-charge session is often 60–80% of the charger's peak rating — especially from a cold battery or above 60% SoC. For the most accurate estimate, enter the charger's realistic average power rather than its nameplate peak.

How do I find my EV's battery capacity in kWh?

Check your owner's manual, the manufacturer's spec page, or the window sticker. Manufacturers publish the usable (net) capacity, which is slightly lower than the gross capacity — use the usable figure for this calculator. Common examples: Nissan Leaf (40 kWh), Chevrolet Bolt (65 kWh), Tesla Model 3 SR (57 kWh), Tesla Model 3 LR (82 kWh), VW ID.4 (77 kWh). The energy reading on your car's display in kWh tells you how much is stored, not the total capacity.
