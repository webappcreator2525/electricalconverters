VA to Watts Formula

W = VA × PF

Example: 2,000 VA × 0.85 PF = 1,700 W

kW = (VA × PF) ÷ 1,000

Example: 2,000 VA × 0.85 PF = 1.7 kW

The power triangle shows three related quantities: apparent power (VA), real power (W), and reactive power (VAR). The power factor is the cosine of the angle between apparent and real power: PF = W ÷ VA. A PF of 1.0 means all apparent power is doing useful work; a lower PF means some power is being stored and returned by reactive components (inductors, capacitors) rather than consumed.

VA vs Watts: What's the Difference?

Volt-Amperes (VA) — Apparent Power

VA is the total power drawn from the supply, combining both real power (doing useful work) and reactive power (stored and returned by inductors and capacitors). Cables, fuses, circuit breakers, UPS units, and transformers must all be sized to handle the full VA load — not just the watts.

VA = W ÷ PF
Watts (W) — Real Power

Watts measure the actual work performed: generating heat, light, or mechanical motion. This is what your electricity meter measures and what you are billed for. For resistive loads like heaters and incandescent bulbs, VA and watts are identical. For inductive loads (motors, transformers, UPS), watts are always less than VA.

W = VA × PF

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I convert VA to watts?

W = VA × PF, where PF is the power factor (a number between 0 and 1). For a 1,500 VA UPS with a power factor of 0.6: W = 1,500 × 0.6 = 900 W. For resistive loads (PF = 1), VA equals watts exactly.

Is VA the same as watts?

No. VA (volt-amperes) is apparent power — the total power drawn from the supply, including reactive power from inductive or capacitive loads. Watts is real power — the portion that does actual work. They are equal only when the power factor is 1.0 (purely resistive loads like incandescent bulbs and simple heaters). For motors, UPS systems, and most electronics, PF < 1, so VA > watts.

How many watts is a 1500 VA UPS?

It depends on the UPS power factor. Older UPS units: PF ≈ 0.6 to 1,500 × 0.6 = 900 W. Modern online double-conversion UPS units: PF ≈ 0.9 to 1,500 × 0.9 = 1,350 W. Check the UPS datasheet for the rated power factor.

What power factor should I use for my calculation?

Common values: 0.6 for older UPS units (offline/line-interactive type), 0.7–0.8 for industrial motors, 0.8–0.9 for modern UPS (double-conversion), 0.9–0.95 for large commercial HVAC, 1.0 for resistive loads (heaters, incandescent lights). When in doubt, use 0.8 as a conservative estimate.

How do I find the power factor of my device?

Check the device datasheet or the label on the back panel — it is often listed as "PF" or "cos φ". If not listed, use a power meter (such as a Kill-A-Watt device) to measure VA and watts simultaneously; PF = W ÷ VA. For UPS systems, the power factor is always specified in the product specifications.
